# Time-Windows boundaries


The initial approach I provided calculates the time window index based on the division of the timestamp by the time window duration, effectively making the left boundary inclusive and the right boundary exclusive naturally, because the calculation floors the result to the nearest whole number. When a timestamp falls exactly on the boundary of a time window, it is included in the window that starts with that timestamp, not the previous one, aligning with the requirement that the left boundary is inclusive and the right boundary is exclusive.

To clarify:

Left boundary inclusive: This means that if a timestamp falls exactly at the beginning of a time window, it is considered part of that time window. Our calculation method inherently supports this because when dividing the timestamp by the time window duration, it counts as the start of the new window.

Right boundary exclusive: This means that if a timestamp is exactly on what would be the "end" of a time window, it actually belongs to the next time window. The division method naturally accounts for this, as the calculation assigns it to the next window without any additional adjustments needed.

Given this behavior, the provided solution indeed respects the requirement that the left boundary of the time-window is inclusive, and the right boundary is exclusive. The calculation for which window a timestamp falls into does not need to change because it inherently respects these boundaries due to how integer division works in this context.

To further illustrate, consider a time window duration of 15 minutes (900,000 milliseconds):

A timestamp at 09:00:00.000 (the start of a window) is included in the window starting at 09:00.
A timestamp at 09:14:59.999 (just before the next window) is also in the 09:00 window.
A timestamp at 09:15:00.000 marks the start of the next window, not the end of the 09:00 window, thus respecting the exclusive nature of the right boundary.
